Digital Transformation Is Dead — Long Live Product Thinking

The phrase "digital transformation" has been so abused it's almost meaningless. Here's the honest reframe that actually helps businesses change.

The Transformation Lie

Every company says they're "digitally transforming." Almost none of them are. What they're doing is buying enterprise software, hiring consultants, and running change management programmes that produce org charts, not outcomes.

Real transformation is measurable. It shows up in time-to-market, customer satisfaction scores, unit economics, and the ability to change direction when the market shifts. If your transformation programme can't point to those metrics after 18 months, you're not transforming — you're decorating.

💡

The Uncomfortable TruthMost "digital transformation" initiatives fail not because of technology — but because they're trying to preserve existing business models with new tools rather than building new models that technology makes possible.

What Product Thinking Actually Means

Product thinking is not about having a Product Manager. It's a set of organisational beliefs:

  • Problems before solutions — deeply understand the user problem before proposing technology
  • Outcomes over output — measure success by customer and business outcomes, not features shipped
  • Continuous discovery — treat assumptions as hypotheses to be tested, not facts to be implemented
  • Cross-functional ownership — engineers, designers and data analysts in the same room making decisions together

The Three Mindset Shifts

1. From Projects to Products

Projects have end dates. Products don't. When you run technology as a project, you optimise for delivery milestones. When you run it as a product, you optimise for continuous value delivery. Most enterprise IT departments are still running projects. That's the core of the problem.

2. From IT Cost Centre to Business Capability

Technology is not overhead to be minimised. In every competitive market today, technology is the primary source of competitive advantage. Companies that treat their technology teams as cost centres are already losing — they just haven't seen the results yet.

3. From Waterfall to Continuous Delivery

Shipping software quarterly is not a competitive strategy in 2025. The companies winning are shipping multiple times per day, learning from real user behaviour, and adjusting weekly. This requires not just technical change (CI/CD pipelines) but cultural change (comfort with imperfection, celebration of learning from failure).

How to Actually Start

The biggest mistake is trying to transform everything at once. Pick one product — ideally one that has a measurable customer outcome, a small cross-functional team, and executive sponsorship — and run it as a true product team for six months. Use the results to make the case for the next team, and the one after that.

"Transformation doesn't happen in a strategy deck. It happens in the daily decisions of individual teams. Start with one team, do it properly, then let the results speak."
— Yashrab Unnisa , CEO, TechEchos Innovation

The Mistakes We See Constantly

  • Buying enterprise platforms before understanding what problems they solve
  • Measuring success by technology deployed, not outcomes delivered
  • Separating technology strategy from business strategy
  • Expecting transformation without giving teams the autonomy to make decisions
  • Hiring a "Chief Digital Officer" and expecting the reporting line to do the work

Key Takeaways

What To Remember
  • Transformation is measurable — if you can't measure it, it's not happening
  • Product thinking is a set of beliefs, not a job title
  • Outcomes over output: measure by customer and business results
  • Start with one team, prove the model, then scale
  • Technology strategy and business strategy must be the same strategy
  • Autonomy without accountability is chaos; accountability without autonomy is theatre
