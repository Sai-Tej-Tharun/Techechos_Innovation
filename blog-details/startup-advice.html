Why Startups Fail at Technology — The 7 Patterns We See Over and Over

After working with 150+ startups, we see the same failure patterns repeat with remarkable consistency. Not bad luck — bad decisions that feel like good decisions at the time.

Pattern 1: The Wrong Co-Founder

The most common failure we see — and the one founders least like to hear — is the wrong technical co-founder. A non-technical founder who pairs with a technical co-founder who can't say no to scope creep, can't communicate timelines honestly, or prioritises technical elegance over shipping will burn their runway before finding product-market fit.

The best technical co-founders are opinionated about what not to build. They understand that the enemy of a startup is complexity, not competition.

💡

The Right QuestionBefore hiring a technical co-founder, ask: "Tell me about a feature you decided NOT to build and why." A great answer tells you more than any coding test.

Pattern 2: Building Before Validating

We've reviewed codebases that represent six months and $200,000 of engineering time — for products that had never been tested with a real user. The lean startup principle is not a Silicon Valley fad. It's the most effective form of risk management available to a pre-PMF company.

Pattern 3: Premature Optimisation

We've seen startups spin up Kubernetes clusters, implement event-driven microservices, and set up multi-region deployments — before their first 100 users. The result is always the same: engineering resources that should have been spent understanding user needs are instead spent maintaining infrastructure complexity that won't be needed for two years.

"Make it work. Make it right. Make it fast. In that order, and only after the previous step is genuinely complete."
— Kent Beck (still applies in 2025)

Pattern 4: The Vendor Lock-In Trap

Choosing a vendor because they have a generous startup credit programme is fine — if you're choosing a commodity service like cloud compute or email. Choosing a vendor for your core business logic because they gave you $50K in credits is building on sand. We've helped three startups rewrite core features that were baked into third-party AI platforms that changed their pricing or API.

Pattern 5: Hiring Seniors Too Early

Senior engineers at startups are only valuable if the startup is ready to give them real problems to solve. A senior engineer stuck in a company that doesn't know what to build is an expensive, frustrated, soon-to-leave engineer. The sweet spot for senior hires is when you have clear product direction and need to raise the engineering quality bar — not before.

Pattern 6: The Monolith vs Microservices Debate

Start with a monolith. Always. Extract services when you have a specific scaling problem that the monolith can't solve, not before. The engineering teams who've ignored this advice and started with microservices from day one have all, without exception, ended up with a distributed monolith — the worst of both worlds.

✅

The RuleIf you can't explain the specific scaling bottleneck that justifies extracting a service, you're not ready to extract a service.

Pattern 7: Ignoring Security Until It's Too Late

Security is not a feature you add at Series B when you're talking to enterprise clients. Every startup that handles user data is a target. We've seen pre-revenue startups get breached, lose their user base, and never recover. Basic security hygiene — dependency scanning, secrets management, least-privilege IAM, encrypted data at rest — costs almost nothing to implement from the start.

What Actually Works

  • Validate before you build — user interviews are cheaper than engineering sprints
  • Start with a monolith, a simple deployment pipeline, and a single database
  • Ship to real users in the first 30 days, no matter how rough it is
  • Measure everything from day one — product analytics are free
  • Keep your infrastructure boring — use managed services for everything non-differentiating

Key Takeaways

What To Remember
  • The best technical decisions at a startup are the ones not made yet
  • Validate before you build — always, without exception
  • Premature optimisation kills startups as surely as insufficient runway
  • Monolith first, microservices when you have a specific scaling problem
  • Senior engineers need clear problems to solve to be worth the salary
  • Security from day one costs nothing; security retrofitted at Series A costs a lot
