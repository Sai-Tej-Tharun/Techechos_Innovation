Design Systems in 2025 — Why Yours Is Probably Costing You More Than It Saves

Design systems are supposed to speed you up. Most of them slow you down. After building 4 systems from scratch and auditing a dozen more, here's what separates the ones that work.

The Design System Paradox

We've audited 14 design systems at companies ranging from 10-person startups to 2,000-person enterprises. Our consistent finding: the bigger the investment in the design system, the more likely teams are to work around it.

The paradox is that systems built to create consistency often create more inconsistency — because designers and engineers route around components that don't meet their needs, creating a shadow design system of one-off implementations that never make it back into the system.

💡

The Core ProblemMost design systems are built for the people who maintain them, not the people who use them. A system that's a joy to maintain but painful to consume will always be worked around.

Why Most Design Systems Fail

After reviewing 14 systems, these are the failure patterns we see most consistently:

  • Component-first thinking — building components before establishing the design language means components will be rebuilt when the language changes
  • Figma-only existence — a design system that doesn't have a code implementation is a style guide, not a system
  • No adoption metrics — if you don't measure how much of your product is built with system components, you're flying blind
  • Over-specification — components with 47 variants solve problems that don't exist yet and create maintenance debt
  • No consumer feedback loop — systems built without continuous input from the designers and engineers who use them drift out of sync with reality

Getting the Foundation Right

The foundation must come before the components: colour, typography, spacing, shadow, border radius, and motion. These are the decisions that propagate through every component you'll ever build. Getting them wrong early means reworking hundreds of components later.

We follow a specific sequence:

  1. Define the colour system (semantic tokens, not raw values)
  2. Choose and specify typography at every scale point
  3. Establish the spacing scale (we use 4px base, not 8px)
  4. Define interactive states (hover, focus, disabled, loading, error)
  5. Build the 5 primitive components (Button, Input, Text, Icon, Link)
  6. Compose complex components from primitives only

Design Tokens Are Non-Negotiable

Design tokens transform your design system from a static library into a themeable, maintainable system. Every visual decision — colour, typography, spacing, shadow — should be expressed as a named token, not a hardcoded value. This is what makes dark mode, white-labelling, and global brand updates achievable without touching individual components.


:root {
  
  --color-purple-600: #7B2FBE;
  --color-orange-500: #F97316;
  
  
  --color-brand-primary: var(--color-purple-600);
  --color-interactive: var(--color-brand-primary);
  --color-surface-card: var(--color-neutral-900);
  
  
  --button-bg: var(--color-interactive);
  --button-radius: var(--radius-full);
}

Accessibility Isn't Optional

WCAG AA compliance is not a nice-to-have. In most markets it's a legal requirement. More importantly, accessible components are better components for everyone — they have better keyboard navigation, clearer focus states, and more robust interactive patterns.

✅

Our RuleEvery component in the Orbit system must pass automated accessibility testing (axe-core) AND manual keyboard and screen reader testing before it can be considered complete. No exceptions.

The Governance Model Nobody Talks About

The biggest predictor of design system success isn't the quality of the components — it's the governance model. Who can contribute? How does a new component get approved? How are breaking changes communicated? How is deprecation handled?

We use a federated model: a core team of 2 maintains the system and sets quality standards. Any product team can contribute components through a defined proposal and review process. The core team reviews all contributions for quality and API consistency, but doesn't own all the components themselves.

Key Takeaways

What To Remember
  • Build for consumers, not maintainers — adoption is the only success metric
  • Foundation first, components second — always
  • Design tokens enable theming, white-labelling, and dark mode without component changes
  • Accessibility built in from the start is 10x cheaper than retrofitting
  • Measure adoption — if you don't know how much of your product uses the system, you can't improve it
  • A federated governance model scales better than a central ownership model
